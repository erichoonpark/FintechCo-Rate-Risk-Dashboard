## 1. Current Risk Environment

The Rate Risk Index ended March 2026 at +1.055, its highest reading in over two years and a sharp reversal from the mild easing trend that held through the prior six months. While this level does not yet breach the +1.5σ alert threshold, the velocity of the move — jumping from near-zero in February — warrants close attention heading into Q2. If this trajectory continues into April, we could cross into elevated tightening territory, which historically has compressed lending margins and increased borrower stress across our portfolio.

---

## 2. Key Stress Periods

**August 2019 (−1.540):** The Fed made its first emergency-style rate cut in over a decade as trade war fears rattled markets and Treasury yields collapsed. For a fintech lender, sudden easing of this kind signals deteriorating credit demand and often precedes a rise in delinquencies as the macro outlook weakens.

**March–April 2020 (−3.745 / −2.786):** The most severe readings in the entire dataset reflect the COVID-19 shock response. The Fed slashed rates to zero, inflation collapsed, and Treasuries cratered simultaneously. For payments businesses, transaction volume dropped sharply; for lending, credit risk became nearly unquantifiable overnight. This is the clearest example of how a crisis-driven easing spike can be more operationally disruptive than a tightening cycle.

**2022 Tightening Cycle (March through October):** Six months breached the +1.5σ threshold — four of them above +1.5σ, with June 2022 peaking at +2.460. Inflation drove the early flags; aggressive Fed hikes and surging Treasury yields sustained them. For FinTechCo, this period would have raised our cost of capital, tightened consumer credit availability, and increased prepayment risk on fixed-rate loan products. The October 2022 peak (+2.105) was driven equally by Fed action and a significant Treasury sell-off, reflecting the market's loss of confidence in a soft landing.

---

## 3. What to Watch (Next 30 Days)

The April CPI release (due mid-month) is the single most important data point right now — a second consecutive upside surprise would likely push our index through the +1.5σ threshold and force a reassessment of our Q2 credit pricing assumptions. Alongside that, any Fed communication shifting tone toward resumed hikes, particularly in the May FOMC statement or Chair press conference, should be treated as a direct signal to revisit our liability hedging posture. Finally, monitor the 10-year Treasury yield closely; if it approaches or breaches 5%, experience from late 2022 shows that consumer loan demand softens quickly and our cost of new funding rises in a way that compresses net interest margins within 60 to 90 days.
